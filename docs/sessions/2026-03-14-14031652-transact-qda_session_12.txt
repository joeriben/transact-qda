     Plan: transact-qda Manual for Outsiders

     Context

     transact-qda has 12 design sessions (32k lines) documenting its
     epistemological, methodological, and architectural foundations. There is no
      single document that introduces the project to outsiders — qualitative
     researchers familiar with Situational Analysis and QDA tools (ATLAS.ti,
     MAXQDA) who want to understand what transact-qda does differently and why.

     Language: English
     Audience: SA/QDA researchers
     File: docs/manual.md

     Structure

     The manual builds from why (the problem) through what (the foundations) to
     how (the platform). Each section should be self-contained enough to read
     selectively, but the argument builds cumulatively.

     1. What Is transact-qda?

     One-paragraph elevator pitch. QDA platform grounded in Situational Analysis
      (Clarke) and transactional philosophy (Dewey/Bentley). At the intersection
      of STS and Erziehungswissenschaft (Sünkel). Open-source, self-hosted.

     Source: Session 00 opening prompt, DEVLOG header

     2. The Problem: Why Another QDA Tool?

     Three concrete critiques of existing QDA software:

     a) Entity-first ontology. ATLAS.ti/MAXQDA presuppose fixed entities (codes,
      categories) that are then applied to data. But in SA: elements are
     constituted through relations, not pre-given. The tool imposes an
     inter-actional ontology where the method demands a trans-actional one.

     b) Relations as second-class citizens. In existing tools, a "link" between
     two codes is an edge — it has no inscription, no history, no provenance, no
      designation chain. You cannot relate to a relation. But SA's relational
     analysis (and Sünkel's Eta-Beziehung / meta-relations) require relations
     that are themselves full analytical objects.

     c) Paper's unacknowledged actancy. Clarke's method was developed for paper.
      Paper enforces: labels ARE things (entity-fixation), lines are NOT
     nameable objects (relation-poverty), messy→ordered sequence is materially
     determined. Clarke's procedure is a method-for-paper theorized as
     method-as-such. Digital removes these material constraints — making a
     different SA possible.

     Sources: Sessions 00, 10, 11 (material-mediality argument);
     00_epistemological_foundations.md §11 (Clarke critique)

     3. Foundations

     3a. Transactional Ontology (Dewey/Bentley)

     The basic unit is the naming act (event), not the entity. Dewey/Bentley's
     Knowing and the Known (1949): trans-actions, not inter-actions between
     pre-given entities. Entity/relation is not intrinsic but emerges
     perspectivally.

     A naming is a superposition — neither entity nor relation until observed
     from a perspective. The same naming can appear as entity on one map,
     relation on another, silence on a third.

     Sources: 00_epistemological_foundations.md §1; Session 00 (superposition
     concept); Session 01 (researcher-as-naming)

     3b. The Designation Gradient (CCS)

     Dewey/Bentley's naming taxonomy:
     - Cue: Pre-linguistic signal. Something registered but not yet named.
     - Characterization: Provisional naming. Functional but loose.
     - Specification: Analytical precision. Never final.

     Bidirectional, append-only. "Messy vs. Ordered" is not a mode — it is the
     aggregated designation state. A naming IS its designation history (not HAS
     a history).

     Sources: 00_epistemological_foundations.md §3; Session 01 (designation
     tables); Session 02 (messy/ordered as gradient)

     3c. Clarke's Situational Analysis — And Its Limits

     Present Clarke's 5 formal properties of "situation" and 12 heuristic
     categories. Then the critique:

     - Pre-analytical ontologization: requires knowing what is
     "human"/"nonhuman" before analysis constitutes these distinctions
     - Performative contradiction: claims Foucault but presupposes the
     anthropological subject Foucault deconstructs
     - The ordered map destroys the analytical capacity the messy map creates
     - Inductive exhaustion (200 pairwise comparisons) vs. abductive Gestalt
     (constellational perception)

     Sources: 00_epistemological_foundations.md §11–12; Sessions 10, 11

     3d. The Unmarked (Spencer-Brown / Luhmann / Rancière)

     Every naming draws a distinction → marked space + unmarked space. The
     unmarked has structure:
     1. Named exclusion (countable but excluded)
     2. The uncounted (structurally uncountable within the frame)

     Two observation levels: observed unmarked (in the data) vs. analytical
     unmarked (in our observation). Clarke's categories fix the analytical
     counting frame.

     Resolution: Inquiry as dual operation — re-naming observed namings (CCS
     movement) AND naming observed unnameds (constitutive act, grounded through
     constellation of participations).

     Sources: Session 11; 00_epistemological_foundations.md §12

     3e. Provenance: Two Orthogonal Dimensions

     such thing as context" → all material belongs in the corpus.

     4. The Data Model: Three Tables

     The philosophical foundations translate into a minimal, rigorous schema.

     4a. Namings

     The virtual object. Pure potentiality. Has an inscription (the mark of its
     constitution) but no inherent kind. Neither entity nor relation
     intrinsically.

     namings(id, project_id, inscription, created_by, created_at, deleted_at,
     seq)

     4b. Participations

     Undirected bonds. Symmetric, co-constitutive. A participation IS itself a
     naming. Enables meta-relations: relations relating to relations (Sünkel's
     Eta-Beziehung).

     participations(id [= a naming], naming_id, participant_id)

     4c. Appearances

     Perspectival collapse. This is where entity/relation/silence EMERGES.
     Direction, valence, and properties exist only under a perspective.

     appearances(naming_id, perspective_id, mode, directed_from, directed_to,
     valence, properties)

     Modes: entity, relation, constellation, process, silence, perspective

     4d. Naming Acts (Unified Stack)

     A naming IS its stack of acts. Each act carries changes across multiple
     dimensions: inscription, designation, mode, valence. NULL = unchanged. The
     current state = latest non-NULL value per dimension.

     naming_acts(naming_id, seq, by, inscription, designation, mode, valence,
     memo_text, linked_naming_ids)

     This replaces separate inscription/designation tables — mode and valence
     changes are first-class naming acts.

     Sources: Types in src/lib/shared/types/index.ts;
     migrations/002_transactional_ontology.sql through 009_naming_acts.sql

     5. The Three-Layer Hierarchy

     1. Data structure (DB tables) = ground truth. This is what IS.
     2. List = privileged representation. Complete, dimensionless. The Namings
     page — closest a UI can get to the data structure. Primary workspace.
     3. Canvas/Maps = derivative projections. Cognitively helpful but
     epistemically subordinate.

     No operation may exist only on the canvas. No analytically relevant state
     may be visible only on the canvas. The list must show everything the canvas
      shows.

     Key insight from Session 09: Core operations (designate, rename, relate,
     withdraw, stack) operate on the naming ITSELF, not on its appearance. The
     Namings page is not derivative — it is the primary workspace.

     Sources: 00_epistemological_foundations.md §4, §9; Session 09 (critical
     correction)

     6. Working With transact-qda

     Practical walkthrough of the analytical workflow, grounded in the concepts
     above:

     6a. Situational Mapping

     - Creating a map (= a naming that serves as a perspective)
     - Adding elements (= naming acts with initial designation 'cue')
     - The messy phase: designation profile shows aggregate cue-level
     - Relating elements: creating participations (relations as namings with
     their own stacks)
     - Phases as sub-perspectives (emergent clustering, not pre-given
     categories)
     - The ordered phase: designation profile shifts toward
     characterization/specification

     6b. The Grounding Layer

     - Documents (text, images) as corpus material
     - Text annotation (CSS Custom Highlight API, character-offset anchors)
     - Image annotation (rectangle regions, normalized [0,1] coordinates)
     - In-vivo coding: create naming during annotation (type name → create +
     annotate)
     - Codes as derived view from maps — not a separate ontological domain

     6c. The AI Co-Researcher

     - AI as naming in the data space (ai_namings table)
     - AI produces cues; only the researcher designates (cue → characterization
     = acceptance)
     - Discussion at the cue: multi-turn dialogue, rewrite, withdrawal — not
     binary accept/reject
     - Deconstructive questioning: "What does this naming make uncountable?" not
      "Did you consider nonhuman actors?"
     - Full map context visibility — AI sees the relational fabric, not just
     individual elements

     6d. Collaboration

     - Project members with role hierarchy (owner > admin > member > viewer)
     - Every user becomes a researcher-naming in the data space
     - All acts attributed via naming, not user-id — full analytical provenance

     7. Design Commitments Beyond Clarke

     Brief section on the three commitments:
     1. Categories as relational differances (questions about distinctions, not
     slots to fill)
     2. No enforced procedure — diffractory methods (Barad): map iterations as
     superpositions, not linear progress
     3. Theories as visible namings on the map (not invisible lenses)

     Sources: 00_epistemological_foundations.md §11 "Design Commitments"

     8. Technical Overview

     Brief section (for researchers who want to run/deploy it):
     - SvelteKit 5, PostgreSQL, provider-agnostic AI client
     (OpenRouter/Anthropic)
     - Self-hosted, no cloud dependency
     - No ORM (direct SQL preserves transactional data model)
     - API keys in *.key files (gitignored)

     Writing Approach

     - Tone: Academically precise but accessible. Not a paper — a manual. Define
      terms on first use.
     - Length: ~3000-4000 words. Dense but scannable.
     - Citations: Reference Dewey/Bentley, Clarke, Barad, Spencer-Brown/Luhmann,
      Rancière, Sünkel, Haraway where relevant — but as brief markers, not
     literature review.
     - Diagrams: ASCII tables for the data model and provenance matrix. No
     images needed.
     - Code: Minimal — show table schemas as pseudocode, not full SQL.

● Good question — and your intuition that it might be a pseudo-problem is
  largely correct, but for interesting reasons.

  1) Logical Analysis

  The asymmetry you describe is real at the graph-theoretic level: in standard
  graphs, edges are dyadic (connect exactly 2 nodes), while nodes have unbounded
   degree. But this is an artifact of the formalism, not a deep ontological
  fact.

  Three levels of resolution:

  a) Formal reducibility: Any n-ary relation can be decomposed into binary
  relations + auxiliary entities (this is provable in first-order logic via
  reification). But this is lossy: "A, B, C meet together" ≠ "A meets B" + "B
  meets C" + "A meets C". The co-presence is lost — the event-as-unit dissolves.

  b) Your model already solves this. The 3-table structure (namings →
  participations → appearances) is precisely a hypergraph in disguise. A naming
  with 5 participations IS a 5-ary relation. The participation table is the
  incidence matrix of a hypergraph. There is no dyadic constraint.

  c) The deeper dissolution: In your transactional ontology, namings (relations)
   can themselves be participants in other namings. This means the
  entity/relation distinction is not ontologically primitive — everything is an
  event that can connect to other events. The asymmetry (many-to-2 vs.
  many-to-many) only exists if you hold the entity/relation distinction fixed.
  You don't.

  So: pseudo-problem, but an instructive one. It reveals that the classical
  graph formalism smuggles in an ontological commitment (dyadic ties between
  pre-given entities) that your framework explicitly rejects.

  2) Treatment in SNA

  SNA has struggled with exactly this:

  Dominant paradigm (Wasserman & Faust): Dyadic ties, adjacency matrices.
  Everything is reduced to pairs. This works for simple cases but systematically
   loses n-ary structure.

  Two-mode / affiliation networks (Breiger 1974, "The Duality of Persons and 
  Groups"): Exactly your structure — actors connected to events, events
  connected to actors. Breiger showed that persons are constituted by their
  group memberships and vice versa. This is the closest SNA antecedent to your
  transactional ontology.

  The projection problem: When researchers project a two-mode network (actors ↔
  events) onto a one-mode network (actors ↔ actors), they create dyadic ties and
   lose the event structure. This is widely acknowledged as a problem but done
  anyway for computational convenience. Your model avoids this by keeping the
  event (naming) as first-class.

  Simmel's triad argument: The triad is qualitatively different from the dyad
  (coalition formation, tertius gaudens). This means n-ary relations have
  emergent properties that dyadic decomposition destroys.

  Hypergraph SNA (newer work, e.g., Bretto, Aksoy et al.): Explicitly models
  hyperedges. Still rare in practice but gaining traction in computational
  sociology.

  Upshot for transact-qda

  Your naming → participations structure is already the right solution. The
  "asymmetry" is an artifact of thinking in classical graph terms. In your
  ontology:

  - A naming with 1 participation = a monadic event (self-designation)
  - A naming with 2 participations = classical dyadic tie
  - A naming with n participations = n-ary event (naturally represented)
  - A naming participating in another naming = relation-on-relation (no special
  machinery needed)

  The participation table is the hypergraph incidence structure. No reduction to
   dyads required.
